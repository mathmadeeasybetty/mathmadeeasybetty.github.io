Study Abroad

Your road to a US or Canadian campus

Four things decide whether you make it: timing, process, money, and visa after acceptance. Each card below gives you the overview. Open it and you get the full story.

Application Timeline & University Research

Getting into a US or Canadian university usually takes six months to a year from start to deadline, depending on whether you’re aiming for the main Fall intake or a faster Spring intake, and it runs on a real calendar: applications only open and close within specific windows, not whenever you happen to be ready. This card covers how to build your school list, how many schools to realistically apply to, and what to do while you wait for a decision. Find the season you are currently in below to see exactly where you stand.

In Grade 12 or waiting for EUEE results? You do not have to wait. Preparing has no season restriction, you can start any month: translate your Grade 9–11 transcripts and begin converting your GPA to the US/Canada system now, while your results come. Only submitting the actual application needs to happen within the season windows below.
How to build your shortlist

Don’t apply randomly. Build a mix: a few reach schools (hard to get into), several match schools (your profile fits their typical admit), and 1–2 safety schools you’re confident about. For each one, check three things on the university’s own site: does it accept the English test you plan to take, whether it currently requires or considers the SAT, and does it offer financial aid to international undergraduates? Some schools are famously generous; most are not. Prioritize the generous ones.

Early Decision, Early Action, or Regular Decision?

Many US schools let you choose when you apply within that intake window, and it’s worth knowing the difference before you pick. Early Decision (ED) is binding: if admitted, you must withdraw every other application you submitted, including any financial aid applications elsewhere, and attend that school, so only use it on your genuine first-choice school. With Early Action (EA), you also apply and hear back early, but it isn’t binding, you can still wait and compare offers from other schools. Regular Decision (RD) is the standard, later deadline most students use, and it’s never binding either, so you can wait for every decision to arrive and compare admission and financial aid offers side by side before choosing where to attend. Not every school offers all three; check each one’s own site. If you’re counting on need-based aid, be cautious with ED: you commit to attend before ever seeing your financial aid offer (see the Financial & Aid card below).

How many schools?

Every application costs $50–$90 unless you get a fee waiver (see the Financial & Aid card below), so a focused list beats a long one. Around 6–10 well-chosen schools is a realistic target, each with a real reason it’s on your list.

After you apply

Decisions typically arrive March–April for the Fall intake. See the two-path breakdown further down this card for the Spring intake timeline.

While you wait

Keep attending your Ethiopian university and protect your grades. A low grade now stays on your record and can hurt you if you reapply or go for a Master’s later. Study as if you won’t be accepted, so either outcome leaves you strong.

Application Process

This card walks you through the steps you need to apply, in order: translating your documents, converting your GPA, taking your English test, taking the SAT, writing your essays and collecting recommendation letters, and finally submitting your applications. Follow them one by one and nothing will be missed. Every official website you need is in the Official Links box right after this card.

  1. Translate your documents. Gather Grade 9–11 transcripts while waiting for Grade 12 results, and use a translator with real ATA (American Translators Association) certification, not a service that simply claims to be “certified” without one. This can take time, so start it early. Make sure every official school or government stamp and seal on your certificates gets translated too, not just the printed text; a translation that skips the stamps is considered incomplete.
  2. Convert your GPA. Several credential evaluation agencies exist for this (WES, ECE, and others), and most universities accept any NACES-approved provider. This guide focuses on WES (World Education Services) because it’s the one most widely required and accepted by both US and Canadian universities, so it’s the safest single choice if you’re applying to schools in either country. Create a WES profile and get a Reference Number, then once your ATA-certified translations arrive, send that complete set to WES yourself and receive your official evaluation report back.
  1. Register & pay for your English test. Check which test your target university accepts. Most accept TOEFL or IELTS; some also accept the Duolingo English Test, a cheaper, fully-online alternative you can take from home. Local Ethiopian cards usually can’t pay these sites directly, see Payment Options From Ethiopia below for how to actually get the fee paid.
  2. Take your English Test. TOEFL iBT is tested at St. Mary’s University in Addis Ababa; IELTS through the British Council. Always confirm the exact venue when you book.

    If your school accepts it, the Duolingo English Test is the easier, cheaper option (about $70 instead of $185+). You can take it online from home, and you get your results in about two days. Harvard, Yale, MIT, Stanford, and others accept Duolingo. Check the school’s website to confirm.

  3. Take the SAT, a safety net worth having. The SAT tests Reading & Writing and Math. Many US universities still call it optional, but treat a score as insurance: Harvard, Dartmouth, Brown, Cornell, and Penn all made testing optional at some point and now require it again, and Princeton is following the same path. A score benefits you either way: it strengthens your application if a school stays optional, and protects you if it doesn’t. See Payment Options From Ethiopia below for how to pay the fee.
  1. Write your essays & collect recommendation letters. Your personal essay takes weeks of drafts, so start drafting early, while you prepare for your English test, and write in your own honest voice. US applications also require recommendation letters from your teachers: inside Common App you invite each teacher by email, and the teacher writes and uploads the letter online themselves. You never see or touch it. Give your teachers 3–4 weeks, and help them by sharing a short list of your achievements. Common App also asks for a “counselor”; most Ethiopian schools do not have that role, so your school director or head teacher can act as one.
  2. Submit your applications. Use Common App (one shared form for most US schools) or the university’s own site. Upload your translations, WES report, and test scores.
  1. Wait, and keep moving. Decisions arrive March–April. Until then, focus on your education in Ethiopia and maintain good grades.

DLI (Designated Learning Institution). Your school must be on Canada’s official DLI list, no study permit is issued without a DLI acceptance letter. Verify your school before you apply, not after.

PAL (Provincial Attestation Letter). Most undergrad and college applicants also need this letter from the province, submitted with your study permit application. This isn’t automatic everywhere: some schools issue it only after you accept your offer, others require you to request it yourself. Confirm directly with the school how and when they issue yours. Master’s and PhD students at public DLIs are exempt as of 2026.

Payment Options From Ethiopia

You may have trouble with direct payment from Ethiopia. Here are your options. You only need one test to show English proficiency: IELTS, TOEFL, or Duolingo. The SAT is a separate, optional test, also covered below if you choose to take it.

IELTS. The British Council accepts local bank deposit, no foreign card needed: register online, deposit your fee in Birr to their official account (confirm the current account number on their site before you pay), then email the scanned deposit slip with your name and IELTS reference number within five days.

TOEFL. St. Mary’s University hosts TOEFL testing in Addis Ababa and may have its own local payment arrangement for registrants. Check their official Testing Service page directly to verify current details.

Duolingo. A family member abroad pays as a gift using their own card; none of these sites require the cardholder to be the test-taker.

SAT. Paid directly on the College Board site by credit or debit card or PayPal, there is no local bank deposit option like IELTS has. The cardholder does not need to be the test-taker, so a family member abroad can pay the same way as Duolingo. Budget for the full international fee: $68 base plus a $43 fee for testing outside the US, about $111 total.

Application fee. When you apply to a school, most require an application fee to process your documents, separate from the test fee you already paid. Most universities let you skip it entirely if you show financial need. In the US, this is requested right inside your application portal (Common App’s Profile section): you check a box indicating eligibility, and your school director or teacher confirms it. For Canada, email the university’s admissions office directly to ask about a fee waiver. At $50–$90 per school, applying to 6–10 schools without a waiver can add up fast, so it’s always worth checking first.

* Diaspora sponsorship can pay for any fees or payments in the application process.

Financial & Aid

Tuition and living costs abroad can pass $80,000 per year, but you are not expected to pay that. This card shows where the real money is: the universities and programs that fund international students.

How aid works for international students

The US government does not fund international students. The money comes from the universities themselves and from big scholarship programs.

The 2+2 route: community college first, then transfer

Instead of starting at a four-year university, many international students spend two years at a community college earning an associate degree, then transfer to finish their bachelor’s at a four-year school. Community college tuition for international students typically runs $5,000–$15,000 a year, 50–70% less than a four-year university’s $30,000–$60,000+. Your final diploma comes from the four-year university you transfer into, with no mention of where you started.

Before choosing a community college, confirm two things: it is SEVP-certified (able to sponsor your F-1 visa), and it has a transfer agreement (sometimes called an “articulation agreement”) with universities you actually want to finish at. A strong transfer agreement, plus a good GPA in your two years there, can make admission into the four-year school far more certain than applying there directly as a freshman.

Need-based aid: the main type of support

Need-based aid means the university looks at what your family can honestly pay and covers the difference, including tuition, housing, and food. For example, if a university costs $85,000 per year and your family can contribute $2,000, your aid covers the remaining $83,000. At the most generous universities this money is a grant, not a loan, so you never pay it back. You show your family’s finances through the CSS Profile form explained below.

Need-blind universities: if you get in, you can afford it

A small group of US universities admit international students without looking at their finances at all, then cover 100% of demonstrated need: Harvard, Yale, Princeton, MIT, Dartmouth, Amherst, Bowdoin, Brown, and Notre Dame. Applying for aid does not hurt your admission chances at these schools. They are extremely competitive, but there is zero downside to asking for full aid.

Generous need-aware universities

Many more universities give large aid packages even though they consider your finances during admission, including Stanford, Columbia, University of Chicago, Duke, Williams, Swarthmore, Pomona, and Wellesley (women only). Admitted students with need often receive most or all of their costs covered.

The aid application form: CSS Profile

The CSS Profile is run by College Board, the same official organization behind the SAT, and works much like Common App: one official form, filled out once, sent to every university that you apply to. There is a small fee to process your application, $25 for your first school and about $16 for each one after that. If you cannot afford this fee, email each university’s financial aid office directly, they may waive it for you. The CSS Profile’s own automatic fee waiver does not cover international students, so you need to ask the school directly instead.

Mastercard Foundation Scholars Program

Africa’s largest scholarship program covers full tuition, housing, books, and airfare, and it is built for students with financial hardship. There are 60+ partner universities worldwide, including UC Berkeley, Arizona State, McGill, University of British Columbia, and University of Edinburgh. There is no separate Mastercard application: you apply through the specific university’s own Mastercard Scholars page, and that one application decides both admission and the scholarship together. You can apply to more than one partner university this way.

Free help in Addis Ababa: EducationUSA

The EducationUSA Advising Center at the US Embassy in Addis Ababa gives free guidance on applications, aid, and visas. Their Scholars Program selects a small group of Ethiopian students each year for guided applications, with a fund that covers up-front costs like test fees. You can apply as early as Grade 11.

⚠ Aid policies change from year to year. Before you apply, confirm each university’s current policy on its own financial aid page.

University Acceptance: Visa & Departure

The acceptance letter from your university is the beginning of the final stretch, not the end. From here you’ll lock in your offer, prove you can fund your first year, work through the required US government fees and paperwork, sit a visa interview, and get on the plane. The numbered steps below walk you through that whole sequence in order, including any new fee or requirement added since this page was last updated.

  1. Choose your offer & pay the deposit. If several universities accept you, compare the aid packages and confirm one, usually with a small enrollment deposit by the university’s stated deadline. You attend one school; decline the others politely.
  2. Send financial proof, receive your I-20. The university issues your I-20 only after you show funding for the first year: your aid award letter, bank statements, or sponsor documents. When it arrives, check every detail matches your passport exactly.
  1. Pay the SEVIS fee ($350). This one-time fee registers you in the US system that tracks international students, and it’s paid before your interview, at least one week ahead, so keep the receipt. A diaspora family member can pay it for you from abroad. Use the official site to pay: fmjfee.com.
  2. Apply for the F-1 visa. Complete the DS-160 form online, pay the $185 visa application fee, and book your interview at the US Embassy in Addis Ababa. Interview appointments fill up months ahead. Book your interview as soon as your I-20 arrives, not just a few weeks before you plan to travel.
  3. Prepare your interview folder. Passport, I-20, SEVIS receipt, DS-160 confirmation, acceptance letter, academic records, test scores, and proof of finances matching your I-20.
  1. The interview. It is short and direct, often just minutes. Answer honestly and briefly; know your program, your funding, and your plan. Memorized speeches work against you. The officer has the full right to refuse your visa even though a university accepted you, so be polite and answer every question. The officer’s main goal is to verify that what you told the university is true: that you are a genuine student, and that you can cover your tuition, living, and medical expenses, whether through your family or a scholarship. If refused, you can reapply, and your SEVIS fee stays valid for 12 months.
  2. Pay the New US Visa Fee ($250). US embassies have introduced a new “Visa Integrity Fee” on top of the SEVIS fee and the $185 application fee you already paid. Unlike those two, it is charged only after your visa is approved, not before your interview. Confirm the current amount directly with the embassy, since rollout and pricing have varied by location.
  3. After approval: departure. Book your flight (you can enter the US up to 30 days before your program start date), arrange housing, complete the university’s health and vaccination forms, and note your international-student orientation date.

Applying to Canada instead? The equivalent path is a study permit through IRCC after you receive your Letter of Acceptance. Different forms, same principle: prove enrollment and prove funding.

⚠ Visa fees and procedures change. This is the sequence, but always confirm current requirements on the official US Embassy / IRCC websites before you act.
